Missed opportunity, 5 March 2005
Author: BornJaded (BornJaded@aol.com) from United States

Robert Wuhl should have outsourced his clever idea for 'Open Season' to a screenwriter with more sophistication and a director with more talent. The concept itself -- a malfunction with a Nielsen Ratings-like electronic device leads to a public broadcasting station being mistakenly spotlighted as the highest rated network -- is fecund with possibilities, but Wuhl foils all prospective avenues with a film that is at once scattershot and obvious, so devoid of laughs that the mind begins to numb. The kind of buffoon humor found here is akin to the moronic vulgarity of a 'Police Academy' movie. Gailard Sartain, as a short-fused network owner, contributes to one running gag in which the character compulsively measures his penis, whose length is determined by network ratings. His character's name is "George Plunkett." Other characters are named "Kerwin Keesler" and "Herbert Goodfellow." Wuhl, of course, as the level-headed protagonist, is "Stuart Sain."

You know there's trouble when a comedy is reliant upon funny names, and hell, this is supposed to be "satire." Rather than justifying such aspirations and exploring genuine comedic possibilities, the movie resorts to the tired old format of earnest lampoon, the kind straight out of MAD Magazine, which is Jonathan Swift compared to this movie. Robert Wuhl is a blandly likable presence, and maybe he's redeemed himself with his HBO series "Arli$$" (I've never watched it), but if you've seen the John Ritter comedy 'Stay Tuned,' watching 'Open Season' would not only be painful, but redundant.
